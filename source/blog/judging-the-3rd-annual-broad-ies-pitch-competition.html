Judging the 3rd Annual Broad IES Pitch Competition

Development Community

This past weekend, I had the privilege to judge the undergraduate pitch competition sponsored by MSU's Broad College of Business' Institute for Entrepreneurship. It was really fun to hear the pitches and see the energy of these young entrepreneurs, each with businesses in various stages of development, though I favored the companies that had yet to launch (or were close, but needed just a little capital). Although we choose three winners, each of the finalists had interesting ideas that, given the right approach to design, could be the beginning of a successful business.

The finalists were:

Eightfold Marketing - A team of seven talented students, this company provides exceptional design, marketing, and video production services for small and medium sized businesses in the Lansing area. Started just a few months ago, they have already done a lot of work for several prominent businesses in the Lansing area. The quality of their videos is awesome! The primary reason I didn't vote for this team in the top spot was because they have already started generating revenue and, in my opinion, growing their business is not about proving their concept. Instead, it's about honing their pricing, their service terms, etc.

Carbon Cash (winner) - Carbon Cash is a marketing platform that rewards college students for reducing energy consumption. Their technology captures the amount of energy college students use (at the dorm or apartment level) and awards points via a competition to those students who conserve the most energy. Those points are redeemable for discounts at local retailers. Additionally, there is a benefit to the University or apartment complex where the student resides. As students use less energy, the institutions should see a reduction in their energy bills.

They're piloting the technology at MSU, at which point they'll be able to test whether their scheme works. There is a risk that there isn't a meaningful reduction of energy usage, in which case there is little to no value to the institution or university who is the main paying customer in their business model. There's also risk that students don't play the game or that retailers are unwilling to provide compelling rewards. Still, it's an interesting problem and the team is working hard to reduce the risks. Their pilot is a paid pilot and they have the technical skills to pull it off. It'll be interesting to see what becomes of these guys. Keep your eyes open!

SnapScript (2nd Place) - SnapScript is an app design to help people to remember to take their pills. Using OCR and a notification system, they'll catalog your prescription information and track adherence (how often you actually take your pills, and when). Their business model hinges on being able to collect a lot of data, which they can sell to medical data mining companies. They also work with Apex to provide rebates on refills.

Their founder is using a lean startup approach to developing the business model and wireframes for the project, which is cool. He's already run a lot of experiments to try and figure out messaging, value proposition, and design. Still, the biggest questions in my mind are: a) how valuable is adherence data?, b) how much is a company willing to pay for it, and c) is the market of data mining companies a large enough market to make this worth doing? Additionally, the distinction between users and customers generally makes me nervous. Were it me, I might look for large customers looking to solve this problem (maybe hospital systems?) who are willing to pay for product development, while providing a channel (i.e., patients). It aligns incentives and reduces both the cost and risk of having to satisfy two different stakeholders with different priorities. I don't know whether such a customer exists, though. Still, an interesting concept and they're being tenacious about asking and answering questions, so I expect good things from these guys.

TurtleCell - These guys are a product company. Their main product is an iphone case with headphones that don't get tangled or lost. Check out their campaign on Kickstarter. Their technology is patent-pending and, after trying it, is pretty easy to use. The earbuds pull out and, with the press of an inconspicuous button, retract automatically. The case is also really thin and comes in a variety of colors.

This company is currently in the process of signing a licensing deal with a contract manufacturer/distributor who would be a channel that would allow them to scale up really quickly. They may even have a table at this year's CES. Phone cases are a large industry and their product is cool. Because they're in the process of getting distribution and raising the funds they need, I felt like the prize money would be better used by another team. Still, it's worth it to check them out and support their campaign.

York Apparel (3rd place) - This company sells hats, sweatshirts and other apparel. But, for every product bought, they give a corresponding product to a homeless person (similar to TOMS). Right now, their production is limited to the capacity of their founder and his family, who sew logos, knit hats, sell, and distribute product. However, after being featured in several media spots, they're starting to get some traction. Soon, they'll have to figure out how to scale. It's a cool mission, if not a unique approach, and definitely worth checking out and supporting.

ToU - If there was a team that was most in 'idea' phase, it was this one. Still, the ideas were cool. Their vision was big: a collaborative tool where a student and his prospective roommate could look at a floorplan of their room before arriving to school, arrange furniture virtually, and pay to have it delivered and set up before they arrived. Having just moved myself (and having started a moving/storage company previously), I know how big a pain moving can be. I also know how difficult the logistics of a moving company can be. Or a distribution company. Still, their idea was intriguing and my advice to them: focus. Pick one part of the problem (the app, retail, moving, whichever), and figure out a) how big a problem it really is, b) whether there's an interesting market, and c) how it can be solved. At this point, they're trying to do too much. But, it's also still just a concept. Good luck to them.

Each of the finalists had an interesting story and cool ideas. I'm thankful I was able to participate in the contest and am really stoked to see where their concepts go!
